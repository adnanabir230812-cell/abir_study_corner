## Slide 1

Q

Pathogen:
An entity that can incite disease.

Inoculum:
The pathogen or its parts that can cause infection; that portion of individual pathogens that are brought into contact with the host.

## Slide 4

Virus and Bacteria
No special inoculum is produced by virus and bacteria (except the genus Streptomyces, it produces spores). The individual virus particle and the individual bacterial cell constitute the inoculum.  
 Nematodes
In case of nematodes either the organism itself or its egg act as inoculum.
 Parasitic flowering plants 
Seeds of parasitic higher plants act as inoculum.


---

## Slide 5

Dissemination/dispersal/transmission

transport of spores or infectious bodies, acting as inoculum, from one host to another host at various distances resulting in the spread of the disease

Displacement of a plant pathogen from its place of production or origin to a suitable place where it can grow/establish


---

## Slide 6

EFFECTIVE DISPERSAL

If displacement occurs on a susceptible host and where the environment is suitable, the pathogen can grow and multiply

Ineffective Dispersal

When plant pathogen does not get any medium for its survival. For example, if it falls on a stone


---

## Slide 7

The dispersal of infectious plant pathogens in space occurs through two ways:

Autonomous or direct or active dispersal 
       In this method the dispersal of plant pathogens takes place through soil, seed and planting material during normal agronomic operations. There is no major role of external agencies like insects, wind, water, etc. in this type of dispersal.
Indirect or passive dispersal. 
     in this type of dispersal insects, wind ,water, Human etc.

## Slide 29

Biological transmission are of three types:
 a. Non-persistent (or) stylet-borne: Viruses requiring no incubation time e.g.  Potato virus Y
 b. Semi-persistent (or) foregut-borne 
 c. Persistent : Viruses requiring incubation time
                      e .g.  Potato leaf roll virus
          - circulative  (no virus replication)
          - circulative-propagative  (virus replication)


---

## Slide 30

Non- and Semi-persistent transmission 
• virus is not internalized in the vector 
• virus do not enter the hemocoel and salivary glands of the vector 
 Persistent circulative transmission 
• virus is internalized in the vector 
• virus enters the hemocoel and salivary glands of the vector


---

## Slide 31

Persistent circulative-propagative transmission 
• virus is internalized in the vector 
• virus enters the hemocoel and salivary glands of the vector 
• virus multiplies in the insect vector

## Slide 33

Fungi transmitted by insects:
About 66 species of fungi causing plant diseases are transmitted by more than a 100 species of insects belonging to at least six orders. 
Transmission occurs in various ways with or without injury to the plant.


---

## Slide 34

For example,  spores of Ustilago spp and Botrytis spp are carried from the infected anthers  to the uninfected flowers by pollinating insects such as Bees, wasps. 
Aphids can carry the mycelial fragments and sporangia of Phytophthora infestans on their bodies and leg for the short distance dissemination.

## Slide 35

For example, Cucumber beetle chew the plant parts infected by cucurbit wilt pathogen,  Erwinia tracheiphila and carry the bacterium inside the body and passing on the host in the next season
Seed corm maggot can disseminate the black leg pathogen, Pectobacterium carotovora susp carotovora. 
Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. citri (citrus canker pathogen) is disseminated by citrus leaf miner.
 Mycoplasma transmitted by insects:
Such as Psyllid bug transmits citrus greening mycoplasma and whitely transmits little leaf mycoplasma.

## Slide 40

Dispersal of plant pathogens by nematodes

Some viruses as well as some bacterial and fungal plant pathogens are transmitted by nematodes.

Corynebacterium fascians is disseminated by ectoparasitic nematodes, Aphelenchoides

## Slide 47

Examples: Angular leaf spot of cucurbits caused by Pseudomonas lachrymans. When cucumbers are grown for pickles, the fruits are picked. ## Slide 48

The virus that are spread by animals can also be spread by man. Tobacco Mosaic Virus is known to survive in dry tobacco leaves. Hence, workers who handle tobacco plants in the field are advised not to  smoke while they are working and to thoroughly wash their hands before entering the field.

## Slide 49

iv. Use of contaminated implements: cutting knives etc. helps in transmission of soil-borne pathogens and also the pathogens present in bulbs, tubers, sugarcane setts etc. 
v. Through grafting and budding between healthy and diseased plants: It is the most effective method of distribution of pathogens of fruit trees such as the careless selection of stock and scion for propagating citrus trees.

## Slide 50

vi. Cultural operations:
Hoeing, weeding, pruning carried out by man not only transmit diseases by contact between the plant and contaminated tools but also by providing wounds on plant surface for entry of pathogens that have no capacity to cause direct penetration

## Slide 52

Dispersal by animals:

Cattle feeding on diseased fodder ingest the viable fungal propagules (spores or oospores or sclerotia) and pass out as such in the dung. 
Soil inhabiting fungi especially sclerotia adhere to the hoofs and legs of animals and get transported to other places.


---

## Slide 53

Dispersal by other mammals and birds: 
Pathogens that can persist on clothing or machinery might easily be carried on an animals hair or a birds feathers. 
Examples: 
Rabbits and dogs spread potato virus x in potato crops when they brush past infected plants and break the leaf hairs. 
Birds carry the chestnut blight fungus (Endothia parasitica). Birds also spread the seeds of parasitic higher plants (dodder)
