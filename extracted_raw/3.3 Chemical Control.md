## Slide 7

Fungicides

Fungicide Mobility

Any toxic substance used to kill or inhibit the growth and reproduction of fungi is called fungicides.

Apoplastic movement- Xylem- mobile fungicides are absorbed by the plant and translocated upward to other tissues. 
Symplastic Movement  downward, with carbohydrates in the phloem.
Amphimobile fungicides move throughout the plant. 
Translaminar fungicides can move across and through the leaves, but are not truly systemic, that travels only shorter distances in the plant.

## Slide 8

What is apoplastic and symplastic transport mechanism?

The symplast pathway involves protoplasts, which are found within the living cytoplasm cells. Eg. Carbendazim

The apoplast pathway is a non-living route since the water solution moves in the spaces between cells and along the cell wall (no protoplasts involved). Eg. Benomyl (Bengimidazol)

## Slide 9

What is the difference between Translaminar and systemic?

Translaminar control agents penetrate into the plant tissue and are moved within a plant organ such as a leaf, but do not travel to other parts of the plant. For example, the chemical may move from the upper to lower surface of a leaf. 

Systemic control agents can be effective at sites other than where they are sprayed.

## Slide 10

1. Apoplastic Movement (acropetal):
Move upward from the point of entry through the plant’s xylem. e.g.  Tebuconazole,  Propamocarb, Benomyl.

4. Translaminar fungicides (locally systemic): Absorbed by leaves and move through the leaf to the opposite surface. e.g. Dimethomorph, Iprodione, etc.

2. Symplastic Movement (basipetal):
Move downward, with carbohydrates in the phloem. e.g. Trifloxystrobin, Carbendazim.

3. Amphimobile fungicides (truly systemic):
Move both upward through the xylem, and downward through the phloem. e.g. Fosetyl Al,  Phosphoric acid.
