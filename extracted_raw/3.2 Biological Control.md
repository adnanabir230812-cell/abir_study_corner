## Slide 3

What is biological control?

First coined by Harry Smith in relation to the biological control of insects
Suppression of insect populations by native or introduced enemies
Generic terms
A population-leveling process in which the population of one species lowers the number of another

## Slide 8

Q

Antagonism

Definition:
Antagonism refers to the inhibitory relationship where one organism suppresses or harms another, often by competition, production of antibiotics, or other mechanisms.

Example:
The bacterium Bacillus subtilis produces antibiotics that inhibit the growth of the fungal pathogen Fusarium oxysporum, which causes wilt in many crops.


---

## Slide 9

Q

Antagonist

Definition:
An antagonist is the organism that suppresses or inhibits the growth or activity of a pest or pathogen through various mechanisms like competition, parasitism, or production of harmful substances.

Example:
Trichoderma harzianum is a fungal antagonist that suppresses the growth of plant-pathogenic fungi like Rhizoctonia solani by outcompeting them for nutrients and space or by directly attacking them.

## Slide 10

Mechanisms of biological control of plant pathogens/ How do antagonists act?

Q

Antibiosis – Inhibition of one organism by another as a result of diffusion of an antibiotic
Antibiotic production common in soil-dwelling bacteria and fungi
Example: zwittermicin A production by B. cereus against Phytophthora root rot in alfalfa

Mechanisms of biological control of plant pathogens

Nutrient competition – competition between microorganisms for carbon, nitrogen, O2, iron, and other nutrients
Most common way organisms limit growth of others
Example
P. fluorescens, VITCUS, prevents bacterial blotch by competing with P. tolaasii
