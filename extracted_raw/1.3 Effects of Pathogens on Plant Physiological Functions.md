Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the basic function of green plants: 
It enables them to transform light energy into chemical energy, which they can utilize in all cell activities. 
Photosynthesis is the ultimate source of nearly all energy used in all living cells, plant or animal

## Slide 4

Q

Effects of pathogens on photosynthesis

How do we tell that photosynthesis has been affected?

Pathogens damage leaf tissues – reduced photosynthetic area examples of such diseases
Some diseases destroy chloroplasts and cause their disintegration
Pathogens produce toxins such as tentoxin and tabtoxin which inhibit enzymes involved in photosynthesis
Vascular pathogens reduce chlorophyll content and photosynthesis stops before wilting
Viruses and mollicute cause chlorosis and stunting – photosynthesis is greatly reduced

## Slide 6

Effect on translocation of water and nutrients uptake

Vascular pathogens affect movement of water from the roots

Vascular pathogens also affect movement of nutrients from soil to the leaves – rest of plant deprived of nutrients

If water to leaves is inhibited, leaves cannot function properly – photosynthesis reduced or stopped

Some pathogens affect the integrity or function of the roots

Other interfere with water economy by causing excessive transpiration through leaves and stomata

## Slide 19

Effect of pathogens on host plant respiration

Rate of respiration higher in infected plants
Infected tissues use up reserve carbohydrates faster than healthy tissues
Respirations higher in infected resistant cultivars – higher energy required for production and mobilization of defense mechanisms
Increased respiration has been noticed in cereal rusts, powdery mildews, blast of rice, late blight of potato and many other diseases. 
Level of many enzymes associated with respiratory processes is increased in diseased plants. 
Diseased plants carry out more fermentation than the healthy plants.

## Slide 22

Effect on Transpiration 
Transpiration is the process of water movement through the plant and its evaporation from aerial parts, such as leaves, stem and flowers. 

It is normally observed that the rate of transpiration increases in plants in which the plant diseases infect the leaves (e.g., leaf-rusts, apple scab, powdery mildews, and downy mildews). 

It happens because these diseases result in destruction of the part of the protection devices like cuticle, an increase in permeability of leaf cells, and the dysfunction of stomata.


---

## Slide 23

Transpiration

Increases when plant pathogens infect leaves because of an increase in leaf cell permeability and stomata dysfunction.
Destruction of a considerable portion of the cuticle and epidermis results in an uncontrolled loss of water from the affected areas.
If water absorption and translocation cannot keep up with excessive loss of water, loss of turgor and wilting of leaves.
