## Slide 8

Symbiotic Relationship is a type of parasitism, explain.

Q

Symbiotic relationship refers to any close and long-term interaction between two organisms, including mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism. 

Parasitism is a specific type of symbiosis where one organism, the parasite, benefits by exploiting the host for nutrients, water, or shelter, causing harm to the host. Pathogen like fungi (Puccinia graminis) is a plant parasite. These pathogens attach to, penetrate, infect, and invade host tissues, leading to symptoms such as necrosis, wilting, and reduced yield. 

While all parasitism is symbiotic, not all symbiosis is parasitic, as mutualism and commensalism do not harm the host.

## Slide 60

Q

Successful infection may or may not always result in appearance of symptoms, explain with examples:

A successful infection occurs when a pathogen invades a host plant, establishes itself, and begins to multiply. However, this does not always lead to visible symptoms of disease.

Scenarios Where Successful Infection May Not Cause Symptoms
Latent Infections:
Pathogens infect the host and remain dormant or inactive for some time before symptoms appear.
Example: Apple’s latent virus.


---

## Slide 61

Q

2. Tolerant Host Plants:
Some plants are infected but can resist or tolerate the pathogen's presence without showing symptoms.
Example: Some wheat varieties can host Barley yellow dwarf virus.

3. Environmental Conditions:
The environment may not be conducive to disease development even if infection occurs.
Example: Phytophthora infestans (late blight pathogen) may infect potatoes, but symptoms won’t develop in dry conditions.
