## Slide 27

Effects of  Water Pollution

Oxygen demanding wastes:

Organic matter which reaches water bodies is decomposed by micro-organisms present in water. For this degradation oxygen dissolved in water is consumed.

Dissolved oxygen (D.O.) is the amount of oxygen dissolved in a given quantity of water at particular temperature & atmospheric pressure. The saturation value of D.O. varies from 8-15 mg/l. For active fish species (trout & salmon) 5-8 mg/l of D.O. is required whereas less desirable species like carp can survive of 3.0 mg/l of D.O. Lower D.O. may be harmful to animals especially fish population. Oxygen depletion (

deoxygenation

) helps in release of phosphates from bottom sediments & causes eutrophication (excess plant growth).

## Slide 32

i. Judicious use of agrochemicals like pesticides & fertilizers to reduce their surface run-off & leaching. Avoid use of these on sloped lands.

ii. Use of nitrogen fixing plants to supplement the use of fertilizers.

iii. Adopting IPM to reduce reliance on pesticides.

iv. Prevent run-off of manure. The nutrient rich water can be used as fertilizer in the fields.

v. Separate drainage of sewage & rain water should be provided to prevent overflow of sewage with rainwater.

vi. Plant trees to reduce pollution by sediments & to prevent soil erosion.

vii. For controlling water pollution from point sources, treatment of wastewater is essential before being discharged. It should be properly treated to reduce the BOD, COD levels

upto

the permissible levels for discharge. Advanced treatment for removal of nitrates & phosphates will prevent eutrophication. Before discharge of wastewater, it should be disinfected to kill disease-causing organisms. Proper chlorination should be done to prevent the formation of chlorinated hydrocarbons or disinfection by ozone or ultraviolet radiations should be done.

## Slide 34

Agricultural Pollution

Agricultural pollution refers to biotic & abiotic byproducts of farming practices that result in contamination or degradation of the environment & surrounding ecosystems, and/or cause injury to humans & their economic interests.

Agricultural pollution can be relatively innocuous-for example, the hum of a tractor can be considered noise pollution. Other types of agricultural pollution, however, can cause immense damage, contributing to problems such as eutrophication, climate change, biodiversity loss, & more.

---

## Slide 35

Causes of Agricultural Pollution

Pesticides & Fertilizers:

Once applied, pesticides as well as fertilizers do not disappear completely: some will be absorbed by the plant, while the rest must be processed by the environment. This can lead to runoff & groundwater contamination, drinking water contamination, air pollution through greenhouse gas emissions, & cascading consequences for ecosystems & human health. Additionally, the production of these inputs requires the burning of fossil fuels, that contributes to climate change.

Contaminated Water:

Contaminated water used for irrigation is one further source of agricultural pollution. Much of the water used for irrigation comes from groundwater reservoirs, canals, & rainfall. And while plenty of these water resources is free of contaminants like nitrates & pesticides, other sources are polluted with organic compounds & heavy metals such as mercury, arsenic, lead, & cadmium that can contaminate soil resources & taint crops, as well as pathogens that can contribute to foodborne illness. This, of course, bears consequences for both ecosystem health & public health.

Soil erosion & sedimentation:

Every year,

millions of tons of soil

are lost

to erosion, with

soil particulates

entering waterways & contributing to sedimentation (a process in which particulate matter clouds waterways & blocks sunlight needed by aquatic plants, thereby damaging aquatic ecosystems).

## Slide 36

Causes of Agricultural Pollution

Livestock

-

Manure from livestock also contributes to both air pollution & water pollution. Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFO’s) are a major source of pollution due to the dense concentration of farm animals within a feedlot, as well as the scale of these operations, which generate

millions of tons of manure every year.

This waste is stored often stored in lagoons, contaminating groundwater with antibiotics, nutrients, & other harmful compounds.

Pests & Weeds

-

The introduction of non-native crops to new geographies often causes problems for local ecosystems. In the absence of natural predators, newly introduced species such as plants & weeds often become invasive, leading to greater competition for important food & light resources among native species. Native species may also be more vulnerable to introduced pests & diseases.

Heavy Metals:

Using fertilizers, manure, & other organic wastes containing heavy metals such as arsenic, cadmium, mercury, & lead can also lead to an accumulation of these heavy metals in the soil, while irrigation can also lead to an accumulation of selenium.

When these substances are washed into waterways, leach into groundwater sources, or get absorbed by plants, they are eventually consumed by animals & humans, causing heavy metal toxicity.

## Slide 37

Causes of Agricultural Pollution

Soil Erosion & Sedimentation

-

Intensive farming operations greatly contribute to soil erosion & sedimentation as millions of fertile soils are broken down, degraded, & eroded via storm water runoffs. The

sedimented

soil ends up accumulating in rivers, streams, lakes, oceans, or other land regions, affecting water quality by contaminating it with the agrochemical residues present in the soil. Sedimentation also contributes to the build-up of agricultural pollutants in waterways & other land areas.

Organic Contaminants

-

Manures &

biosolids

frequently contain nutrients, & sometimes contaminants such as personal care products (PPCPs) & pharmaceuticals. These products have been found in human & animal bodies and are believed to have negative health impacts on wildlife, animals, & humans.

Land Management:

Poor land management also leads to an irreversible decline in soil fertility. Profound land management is crucial for keeping agricultural pollution to a minimum level.

Excess Nutrients:

Excess nutrients can have tragic consequences on water quality & the survival of aquatic life. When these nutrients are washed into the water systems, they alter the marine & freshwater nutrient cycles &, consequently, the species composition of the respective ecosystems. The most common consequence is eutrophication, which depletes the water-dissolved oxygen, killing fish & other aquatic life.

## Slide 38

Effects of Agricultural Pollution

Reduction & alteration of wildlife habitats

-

Certain pesticides can harm a wide range of ecologically & economically important organisms, including pollinators, natural predators, birds, & microbial communities. For example, 35% of all food production relies on pollinators, which can be negatively impacted by direct contact with pesticides, as well as residues left on plants. Excess nutrients in waterways spur the growth of “algal blooms,” which deplete the water of oxygen in a process known as eutrophication, thereby contributing to “dead zones” & “fish kills”.

Introduction of toxic chemicals, nutrients, & pathogens

-

A number of agricultural inputs such as pesticides, fertilizers & antibiotics from animal production make their way into drinking water, which can impact human health. Many of these pollutants are known carcinogens & are notoriously difficult to remove from tap water.

Invasive species:

Invasive species introduced through agriculture can harm local vegetation & wildlife, altering ecosystems permanently. In some cases, when native species cannot adapt to novel diseases or introduced pests, or cannot compete with a new species that lack natural predators, they can face extinction.

---

## Slide 39

Effects of Agricultural Pollution

Changes in the hydrologic cycle

-

Water pollution from agriculture can be intensified as a result of practices such as drainage tiling. Although tiling a farm field increases productivity, faster-draining fields reduce filtration, allowing larger amounts of fertilizers & pesticides to enter waterways & in turn worsening problems like eutrophication.

Climate change

-

Among the largest & most complex problems facing our world today, climate change includes not only global warming but also more frequent extreme weather events & shifting dynamics of diseases & pests - all of which affect agriculture profoundly. Agriculture itself is a large contributor to greenhouse gas emissions, which worsen climate change by accumulating in the atmosphere & trapping heat.

Biodiversity Loss:

The persistent use of chemical products in agricultural production degrades & destroys the soils & waters, affecting animals, plants, & wildlife, gradually altering the ecosystems which support biodiversity. Furthermore, the use of pesticides can kill beneficial insects, soil microorganisms, birds, & some rare small species like butterflies, which have far-reaching effects on biodiversity. Since these chemicals remain in the soil for many years, the repercussions on biodiversity are massive.

## Slide 44

Solutions to Agricultural Pollution

Cover Crops & Conservation Tillage

To reduce soil erosion on their fields, many farmers employ a strategy of conservation tillage. By leaving residue on the soil surface, or by planting into residue from the previous crop, intense precipitation events are less likely to cause erosion & runoff.

Cold season cover crops such as oats, vetch, & rye, as well as warm-season cover crops like sorghum-

microbiome

.

Change in Agricultural Practices

Many farms are returning to traditional manure, direct irrigation from local water bodies, & organic means of keeping pest populations in check. But for the process of agricultural pollution to be fully reigned in, there must be a complete shift in how agriculture is practiced.

## Slide 52

Bioaccumulation

Soil pollution can harm humans by

bioaccumulation.

Plants that are grown in lightly polluted soil continuously absorb molecules of the pollutants. Since the plants cannot get rid of these molecules, they accumulate in the plant, causing higher amounts of pollution to exist in the plant than in the soil.

Animals who eat many of these polluted plants take on all the pollution those plants have accumulated. Larger animals who eat the plant-eating animals take on all the pollution from the animals they eat.

Humans who eat plants or animals that have accumulated large amounts of soil pollutants may be poisoned, even if the soil itself does not contain enough pollution to harm human health.

Solutions to Land Pollution

1. Make people aware of the concept of Reduce, Recycle & Reuse.

2. Reusing materials help to reduce the requirement of harvesting resources. The products that cannot be reused can likely be recycled.

3. Reduce the use of pesticides & fertilizers in agricultural activities.

4. Reduce the use of non-biodegradable materials.

5. Avoid buying packaged items as they will lead to creating garbage & end up in the landfill site.

6. Ensure that you do not litter on the ground & do proper disposal of garbage.

7. Buy biodegradable products.

8. Do organic gardening & eat organic food.

9. Create dumping ground away from residential areas.

10.  Avoid littering & properly dispose of waste.

11. Education is key to mitigate the land pollution problem.
