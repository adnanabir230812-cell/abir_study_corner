1. Ecosystem and Ecology

1.1 Key Definitions

Origin: Coined by British scientist A.G. Tansley.

Ecosystem: The basic functional unit of ecology. The interrelation between a living organism and its surrounding environment. The system resulting from the integration of living and non-living factors is called ecosystem.

Ecology: The study of ecosystem (Oikos). The structure and function of nature.

Definitions by Scientists

Odum: The structure and function of nature.

Ernest Haeckel: The study of reciprocal relation between living organisms and their environment.

Smith (1977): Ecology is a multidisciplinary science which deals with organisms and their habitat (place to live) that focuses on the ecosystem.

Important Terms

Crop Ecology: The study of interaction between crop plants and their surrounding environment.

Population: A group of organisms of the same species in a particular habitat that are capable to reproduce.

Community: Assemblage of populations in a particular habitat.

Species: Group of interbreeding populations spread over time covering a region.

Vegetation: Sum total of plant population covering a region.

Flora: All of the plant species in an area.

Fauna: All of the animal species in an area.

Agroecology: Study of ecological processes that operate in agricultural systems.

Components of Ecosystem: Biotic and Abiotic.

Divisions of Ecology

Ecology is divided into: (1) Autecology and (2) Synecology.

Autecology: Concerned with the study of individual animal or plant species.

Synecology: Deals with the system of many species.

2. Scope, Application & Subdivision of Ecology

2.1 Ecology Subdivision Branches

Population Ecology: Study of growth, structure, metabolism, regulation of organisms.

Community Ecology: Ecology of different population in the same habitat and same environmental condition.

Habitat Ecology: Ecology that concerns with the habitat of living organisms.

Palaecology: Deals with environmental conditions and life of the past ages. Concerns with organisms and geological environment of past edges.

Ecophysiology: Physiological adaptation of plants under different ecological conditions. Scope is narrow — how organisms function in their environment.

Cytoecology: Deals with cytological details of the species of plants, animals, soil under different environmental conditions.

Conservation Ecology: Concerns with proper management of environmental resources for human welfare. Scope is broad — how to protect and manage the environment.

Production Ecology: Deals with energy production, process, and energy flow.

Gene Ecology: Deals with the relation of environment to genetic variation.

Molecular Ecology.

2.2 Importance of Studying Ecology

Understanding environment-organism relationship.

Conservation of biodiversity.

Sustainable use of natural resources.

Improving agricultural production.

Environmental protection: help control pollution.

Climate change awareness.

Disaster management.

Promote human health and survival.

3. Principles of Organism-Environment Relationship

Everything influences the life processes of an organism constitutes its environment.

An organism cannot exist in vacuum.

Death is the cessation of life.

Sun is the only source of energy.

Liebig's Law of Minimum: Life activities are influenced by that environmental component which occurs in minimum quantity.

Tolerance limit of an individual — different environmental factors may be different.

Carrying Capacity: Every habitat has potential to support a certain number of organisms.

Energy is neither created nor destroyed. It is only transformed from one form to another.

Life on earth exists in a thin mantle or layer around earth that forms the biosphere.

An ecosystem has producers, consumers, decomposers constituting the biotic component, and life-supporting matter and energy constituting the abiotic component.

4. Light as an Ecological Factor

4.1 Significance of Light for Crop Production

Light is an atmospheric phenomenon.

Plant coverage affects light intensity considerably.

Around 2% of sunlight is utilized for photosynthesis; 10% utilized for other physiological processes, chlorophyll synthesis. Rest of sunlight is lost.

Higher light → higher photosynthesis → better yield.

Light helps in stomata opening and closing.

Light plays an important role in flowering through photoperiod (Short Day Plant: LI(0R)-6400; Long Day Plant: 6800+; Neutral Plant).

Light plays an important role in developing plastids and pigments.

In presence of sunlight, permeability of nutrients is increased.

Different types of microbial activity occurred.

Enzyme activity enhanced with sunlight.

Protein synthesis is induced by high intensity of light.

4.2 Transpiration & Light

Stomata open with K⁺ in presence of light. K⁺ has capacity to retain water making stomata turgid and open.

Light helps starch to convert sugar in guard cells, resulting in increased osmotic pressure, causing inflow of water in guard cells, and stomata opens.

Light stimulates the synthesis of growth regulators and phytohormones.

4.3 Phytochrome and Flowering

Phytochrome: Pr and Pfr are interconverted in the presence of light to initiate flowering.

Pr (inactive) → [660 nm] → Pfr (active); Pfr → [730 nm] → Pr

SDP (Short Day Plant) → Florigen → Precursor of hormone → Flowering hormone

LDP (Long Day Plant) → Florigen → Flowering hormone

4.4 Yield and Yield Formation

Light intensity controls yield. Due to low light (25-75%), yield is reduced.

Poor translocation of assimilates to grain → lower grain yield; unfilled grains, fewer tillers/panicles.

Low light intensity affects: critical flowering, super critical grain formation, panicle initiation (causing lower panicle count), spikelet sterility.

PAR (Photosynthetically Active Radiation): Designates special spectrum of 400-700 nm that photosynthetic organisms use for photosynthesis.

Note: DM - Photosynthesis = Net Photosynthesis (Dry Matter)

Light Compensation Point: Point where respiration = photosynthesis (plant growth stops).

Light Saturation Point: Point beyond which increasing light does not increase photosynthesis further.

Net Photosynthesis: CO₂ use - CO₂ loss by respiration = Net photosynthesis by photosynthesis.

Oil crop, pulse, cereal: -5°C to 5°C base temperature.

4.5 Role of Light: Intensity vs. Duration

Role of Light Intensity:

Affects the rate of photosynthesis.

Amount of light energy received by a plant.

Determines food production, grain formation, biomass accumulation.

Boro rice yields better in high light.

No photoperiod involved.

Role of Light Duration (Photoperiod):

Controls flowering, crop maturity, reproduction.

Length of light period a plant receives in 24 hours.

Determines timing of flowering and maturity.

Short day plant and long day plant classification.

Photoperiod is involved.

Note: Boro rice is cultivated in dry winter season when sunlight availability is comparatively high.

5. Temperature as an Ecological Factor

5.1 Role of Temperature for Crop Production

Cardinal Temperature Points: Maximum, Optimum, Minimum — three critical temperature points that determine growth and development of a plant.

Significance of Temperature

Seed Germination, Dormancy, Fruit Setting, Insect Pest.

Vegetative growth.

Physiological and biochemical processes: Temperature controls the rate of photosynthesis, respiration.

Enzyme activity and metabolism.

Flowering and fruit setting: Many crops need specific temperature for flowering initiation and pollination. Extreme temperature causes flower drop, poor fruit setting.

Nutrient uptake.

Insect-pest infestation: High temperature may increase population of insect pests.

Yield and yield formation: Temperature affects grain filling, fruit development, crop quality.

Crop duration: High temperature speeds maturation.

At determines which species can survive in a particular region.

5.2 Temperature Stress

High Temperature Stress:

Low Temperature Stress: Frost injury (<0°C); Chilling injury (<15°C).

Anatomical Modifications under High Temperature

Disruption of cytoskeleton.

Inhibition of protein and DNA synthesis.

Fragmentation of Golgi complex.

Swelling of mitochondria.

Increase in the volume of nucleolus.

Reduction of enzymatic activity for oxidative phosphorylation.

Disappearance of ribosomes.

Mitigation Strategy for High Temperature Stress

Seed priming: One kind of seed treatment through chemical, mineral. The embryo gets stimulated so seedling grows vigorous. Prim with 0.5% CaCl₂ solution for arresting membrane damage.

Seed hardening.

Foliar application: 2% DAP + 1% MOP solution for avoiding leaf scorching.

Foliar spray of 5% Kaoline for maintaining membrane integrity.

Foliar spray of 0.5 ppm Brassinolite (PAR) for increasing photosynthetic activity.

Foliar spray of 100 ppm Salicylic acid to mitigate stress and promote stem reserve mobilization.

Foliar spray of 10 ppm Naptholene (acetic acid) for controlling premature flower and fruit dropping.

Anatomical Modifications under Low Temperature Stress

Swelling of plasma membrane and micro membrane.

Swelling of thylacoids.

Reduction of mitochondrial cristy and matrix.

Disappearance of ER (Endoplasmic Reticulum) — Rough and Smooth.

Transformation of RER to SER.

Mitigation for Low Temperature Stress

Seed hardening.

Foliar spray of 0.1% Ammonium.

Foliar spray of 2% Ca(NO₃)₂.

2% DAP + 1% MOP.

Foliar spray of Salicylic acid.

5.3 Temperature Requirement of Crops (Paunkias Classification)

Megathermic: Warm habitat. (e.g., Date Palm)

Mesothermic: Neither hot nor cold habitat. (e.g., Wheat, Barley) — cold or temperate.

Microthermic: Low temperature. (e.g., Apple, Peach) — cold or temperate.

Hekistothermic: Cold and alpine region plant. (e.g., Dolonix region)

6. Water as an Ecological Factor

6.1 IWRM — Integrated Water Resource Management

Globally around 97.5% is salty water; 2.6% is fresh water.

Fresh water is consumed 65% in agriculture.

IWRM Definition: A judicious process that promotes the coordinated development and management of water, land, related resources, aiming to maximize economic and social welfare while ensuring environmental sustainability.

IWRM Principles (4 Principles, Dublin 1992)

Fresh water is a finite and vulnerable resource, essential to sustain life, development, and environment.

Water development and management should be based on a participatory approach, involving users, planners, policy-makers at all levels.

Women play a central part in the provision, management and safeguarding of water.

Water has an economic value in all its competing uses and should be recognized as an economic good.

6.2 Adapting Measures for Water Loss (Rice Cultivation)

AWD (Alternate Wetting and Drying): Cultivation for rice.

Organic fertilization.

Introduction of biofertilizer and biostimulants.

Hydrogel application: Combination of pectene, carbohydrate, cytone, selucose.

Exogenous application of silicon and plant growth regulators.

Precision irrigation practice: Technique used to deliver water to crop with pin point accuracy based on environmental condition.

Water equivalents: 1 kg rice → 2500/3000 L water; 1 kg wheat → 1500/2000 L water.

7. Wind as an Ecological Factor

7.1 Definition

Air — horizontal movement of air is wind. About 12 metric ton drain into ocean every year. Currently 44% water is retained in the environment without treatment.

7.2 Direct Effects of Wind on Crop Plants

Desiccation: Air movement removes the leaf boundary layer, increases transpiration, overall water loss from plant. High wind velocities increase transpiration.

Dwarfing: Direct contact with wind and short planting in alpine, coastal dune. When winds are both relatively constant and in the same direction they can permanently alter plant growth.

Deformation: Winds alter the growth of plant.

Plant damage and uprooting, excessive air pressure.

Wind and soil erosion.

7.3 Saving Measures from Wind

Wind break: Made of narrow row of trees/shrubs; small area, reduces wind speed; prevents soil erosion.

Shelter belt (Shrubs): Large area, strong wind; plants protected, long-term benefit.

Planting technique.

Time of planting.

Tolerant variety cultivation.

7.4 Land and Sea Breeze

Land breeze: At night (Land → Sea). Wind blows from land to sea. Cool.

Sea breeze: During day (Sea → Land). Cool, moist.

Land is more heated than sea because land absorbs heat quickly and retains it, but sea absorbs heat slowly and distributes it over a larger depth.

8. Humidity, Atmosphere & Global Environment

8.1 Relative Humidity and Moisture

Absolute humidity: The actual mass of water vapour present in a specific volume of air.

Relative humidity: The percentage of moisture currently in the air compared to the maximum amount the air can hold at a given temperature.

High humidity + transpiration: High wind velocity enhances rate of transpiration, because humid air surrounding the leaves moves away from the plant resulting in decrease of humidity, that increases transpiration.

Very high wind velocity: Vapour from guard cells (leaf cells surrounding stomata) decreases; humidity also decreases, so transpiration increases.

8.2 Layers of the Atmosphere

Troposphere: 0-15 km. Temperature decreases with height. Pressure trapping.

Stratosphere: 15-50 km. Ozone layer absorbs UV rays. Heat trapping (−55°C to -08°C).

Mesosphere: 50-85 km. Ozone, GHGs. Heat trapping. Sun causes distance to increase temperature (120°C).

Thermosphere: Ionization only.

Exosphere: Outermost layer.

8.3 Global Environment Components

Biosphere: Where living organisms are present (org. present).

Atmosphere: Gaseous layer around earth.

Lithosphere: Covered by stone.

Hydrosphere: Covered by water.

Weather: Daily mean atmospheric conditions like rain, temperature. Short-term (daily).

Climate: Mean temperature about 30-40 years over a region. Long-term atmospheric conditions.

10. Greenhouse Effect & Ozone Layer

10.1 Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse: Glass house in temperate area to protect plants from extreme cold. Inside the house remains warm. CFCs, Methane, CO₂ → heat acts as trap heat.

Greenhouse Effect (Global Warming): Sun radiates. Earth surface warms. At night, radiate energy to space, but in troposphere, some gases (CO₂, methane) concentration is higher, trap solar energy. As these gases increase, lower atmosphere gets warmer. This is called greenhouse effect.

11. Ozone Layer Depletion & Acid Rain

11.1 Ozone Layer

O₃ is tri-atomic form of O₂. The layer covers the earth surface and protects earth surface from ultraviolet radiation (Earth sunscreen).

Ozone Layer Depletion: Gradual reduction in the concentration of ozone in the stratosphere in a specific region.

Ozone Hole: Severe, localized thinning of the ozone layer.

CFC as Culprit for Ozone Layer Depletion

Cl acts as culprit for ozone layer depletion:

CFC → [UV] → Cl

Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂

ClO + ClO → Cl₂ + O₂ [Co]

1 [Cl] → 10,000 - 1,000,000 O₃ molecules destroyed.

Ozone Breakdown Reactions

O₃ + UV B → O₂ + [O]

O₃ + [O] → O₂ + O₂

Ozone Formation

O₂ + UV B → [O] + [O]

O₂ + [O] → O₃

11.2 Acid Rain

Acid Rain: N₂, S containing gases (like HNO₃, H₂SO₄) form in atmosphere when they mix in precipitation, forming acid rain.

Chemical Reactions for Acid Rain

N₂ + O₂ → 2 NO

2 NO + O₂ → NO₂

NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃

S + O₂ → SO₂ + O₂ → SO₃

SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄

Effects of Acid Rain on Agro-ecosystem

Nutrient leaching, loss of essential elements, reduce soil fertility.

Excess acidity disrupts nutrient availability.

Reduction of microbial activity.

Reduce crop growth and yield.

Root damage.

Damage plant leaves.

Decreases pH.

Microbial activity destruction.

Fertility and productivity decreased.

13. Biotic Factors Effect on Crop Production

Biotic Factor: Effect from any live activity of living organisms which may cause significant effect for crop production in any way: biotic effect.

Interaction between plants and local animals.

Interaction between plants and microorganisms.

13.1 Biotic Interactions

Grazing, Browsing, Dispersion: Acts as bodyguard of plant. Depends on insect, wind.

Myrmecophily (Ants): Role of animal in pollination: plant depends on insect, wind. Role in dispersal of seed, fruit.

Insect and Carnivorous Nature.

Haustoria - Parasitic Nature.

Liana - action: Vascular plants, rooting in soil, climbing plants. Affect other plants casting shadow, collect atmospheric moisture by roots.

Epiphytes activity: Orchid plant. Uses plant cover, shelter (air). Root has special tissue (velamen). Host plant is a dependent and nutrient (air, it derives haustoria).

Parasitic action: Dodder, haustoria — Cephaleuros. Nutrient (air, it derives) haustoria.

13.2 Mycorrhizal Association

Mycorrhizal Association: Friendly association between fungus and root of higher plant. Both are mutually benefitted. Also called symbiotic association.

Ectotrophic myco: Fungus on the root surface.

Endotrophic myco: Fungus inside the root cells. Absorbs water, mineral from soil. Fungus hyphae acts as a root hair. Supply to roots. Root provides shelter, food to fungus. Both are benefitted.

80% is in this association. Symbiotic association: Nodule formation also.

Mycorrhizal Benefits

Enhance nutrient uptake.

Improve soil fertility.

Increase crop growth and yield.

Helps plants tolerate stress conditions.

Reduce the need for chemical fertilizers.

How Mycorrhiza Influences Root Growth

Through symbiotic association with plant root, the fungal hyphae increase the efficiency of nutrient and water absorption, which promotes healthy root development.

Increase nutrient absorption: mycorrhizal fungi absorb nutrient.

Enhance water uptake.

Increase root surface area.

Protect root from disease.

Stimulating root branching.

15. Biodiversity

Biodiversity: Variability among living organisms, plant, animal, microbes from all sources including terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and ecological complexes.

15.1 Types of Biodiversity

Genetic Diversity: Diversity in plant and animal genetic resources.

Ecological Diversity: Variability among the living organisms in different ecosystems or ecological complexes and include variability among plant, animal.

15.2 Importance of Biodiversity

Provides us food that we eat.

Keeps us healthy.

Supports our ecosystem.

Safeguards nature.

Preserves our cultural ethics and aesthetics.

Maintains ecological balance.

Conservation of natural resources.

Prevention of soil erosion.

15.3 Biodiversity Conservation

Goal: To achieve sustainable development.

01. In Situ Conservation: Conservation of species within natural habitat. Natural ecosystem maintained and protected. Cost effective and convenient. A large number of living organisms can be conserved simultaneously. Example: National park.

02. Ex-Situ Conservation: Involves the breeding and maintenance of endangered species in artificial ecosystems such as zoo, botanical garden.

Strategy for Biodiversity Conservation (In Situ)

All varieties of food, livestock, timber, microbes should be conserved as they might use genetic resources.

All economically important organisms should be identified and conserved.

Unique ecosystems should be preserved as a priority.

Resources should be utilized efficiently.

Poaching and hunting of wild animals should be prevented.

Reserves and protected areas should be developed carefully.

Deforestation should be strictly prohibited.

Environmental law should be followed strictly.

Public awareness.

15.4 Why We Should Conserve Biodiversity

Biodiversity is being lost due to loss of habitat, over-exploitation of resources, climatic change, pollution, invasive exotic species, disease, hunting. Since it provides us with several economic, ethical, and aesthetic benefits we should conserve it by:

Protect natural habitats.

Afforestation and reforestation.

Establish protected areas.

Reduce pollution.

Sustainable use.
