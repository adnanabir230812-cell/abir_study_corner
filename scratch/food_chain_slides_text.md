## Slide 2

Energy flow within an ecosystem always follow the laws of thermodynamics

1. First Law of Thermodynamics (Law of Energy Conservation):

Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only change form.

Example:

Solar energy is converted into chemical energy

by plants (photosynthesis).

Energy is conserved but transforms into different forms (e.g.,

heat,

motion).

2. Second Law of Thermodynamics (Law of Energy Degradation):

During energy transfers, some energy is lost as heat, making energy transfers inefficient.

Example:

At each trophic level, a

portion of energy (around 90%) is lost as heat, leaving only about 10% available for the next level

(10% Rule).

This

limits the number of trophic levels

in an ecosystem.

Eltonian

An ecological pyramid (also called a trophic pyramid,

Eltonian

pyramid, energy pyramid, or sometimes a food pyramid) is a

graphical representation of the relationship between the different living organisms at different trophic levels in an ecosystem

. Charles Elton developed the concept of the pyramid of numbers. Later, G.E. Hutchinson & R. Lindeman developed the idea of the pyramid of energy or productivity.

3 types:

Pyramid of Number.

Pyramid of Biomass.

## Slide 12

Importance of Ecological Pyramid

The ecology pyramid

considers the eating patterns of diverse organisms.

In an ecosystem, the ecological pyramids

represent the number of living organisms.

It

provides some clarity regarding the amount of energy transferred from one trophic level to the next

in a biological system.

It

provides information about the region’s biodiversity.

The ecological pyramid

is built on the interaction between food producers & consumers.

If the

order of the food chain is demolished, the biological pyramid will be destroyed & the entire ecosystem will be severely damaged.

It

aids in maintaining balance & assessing the overall state of a biological environment.

---

## Slide 13

Limitations of Ecological Pyramid

The ecological pyramid is

only applicable in

the case of

simple food chains.

This pyramid makes

no mention of the rate at which energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next.

Even though their relevance to the environment is unsurpassed,

significant energy sources such as

trash & humus are completely disregarded

in the ecological pyramid.

Similar

species residing at different levels of a pyramid are not considered

.

As with the food chain,

several species may inhabit multiple trophic levels

. Therefore, this method

does not account for food webs.

Saprophytes

are not accounted for

in any of the pyramids, despite being an integral aspect of many ecosystems.

These pyramids

only apply to basic food chains, which do not typically exist in nature.

These pyramids

provide little information regarding seasonal and climatic differences.

They do

not consider the notion

of different levels of the same species existing.

## Slide 19

Food Chains and Food Webs

Populations of organisms in a food web are all connected.

A change to one population affects the other populations in the web

An example is if a disease decreases the number of grasshoppers would lead to a decrease in the number of small birds that feed on the grasshoppers. This would cause the higher-level consumers like foxes and hawks to move away or die off.
