ID: 98 | Question: [✦ Repeated ×3] "Symbiotic relationship is a parasitism" — explain with example
=====================================
## Answer

Symbiotic relationship refers to any close and long-term interaction between two organisms, including mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
Parasitism is a specific type of symbiosis where one organism, the parasite, benefits by exploiting the host for nutrients, water, or shelter, causing harm to the host.
Pathogen like fungi (Puccinia graminis) is a plant parasite. These pathogens attach to, penetrate, infect, and invade host tissues, leading to symptoms such as necrosis, wilting, and reduced yield.
While all parasitism is symbiotic, not all symbiosis is parasitic — mutualism and commensalism do not harm the host. For example, mycorrhizae and root nodule bacteria show mutualism — they are symbiotic but not parasitic.

ID: 99 | Question: "All pathogens are parasites but all parasites are not pathogens" — explain
=====================================
## Answer

A parasite is not necessarily a pathogen and a pathogen is not necessarily a parasite.
Some organisms are parasites but do not cause disease — e.g., mycorrhizae or root nodule bacteria derive nutrients from the host but do not harm it (mutualism).
Some pathogens can cause disease without being true parasites — they may act through toxins without living on/in the host.
So the relationship between parasitism and pathogenicity is not absolute.

Pathogen: An agent that can incite a disease.
Most pathogens are parasites because they live in/on the host and derive nutrition while causing disease (e.g., Puccinia graminis causing rust). However, not all pathogens are parasites — some cause disease through toxins without direct parasitic relationship.

## Answer

Epidemic (Epiphytotic): A sudden outbreak of disease periodically over a widespread area in devastatingly severe form causing severe losses. E.g., wheat stem rust (Puccinia graminis tritici), late blight of potato — Irish famine 1845–1846, rice blast — Bengal famine 1943.

Susceptible host
Virulent pathogen
Favorable environment
Time
Human influence — These interactions are described by the disease pyramid

ID: 120 | Question: In epidemiological study disease development differs by monocyclic and polycyclic model — explain with example
=====================================
## Answer

Monocyclic (simple interest model):
Pathogens complete one disease cycle per year
Primary inoculum is the only inoculum for the entire season — no secondary inoculum/secondary infection
Disease progress represented as saturation curve
Equation: X = Q·R·t (Vanderplank)
Examples: Loose smut of wheat, Blackleg of potato (Erwinia caratovora), Verticillium wilt
Polycyclic (compound interest model):
Pathogens go through 2–30 disease cycles in one growing season
Large amounts of asexual spores produced as secondary inoculum at each infection site
Each cycle multiplies inoculum manyfold
Disease progress represented as sigmoid curve
Equation: X = X₀·e^(rt)
Examples: Late blight of potato, downy mildews, powdery mildews, grain rusts, aphid-borne viruses

=====================================
ID: 121 | Question: Analyze the equation X = X₀eʳᵗ of polycyclic disease
=====================================
## Answer

This is the Vanderplank polycyclic model (compound interest disease model):
X = amount of disease at time t
X₀ = initial amount of disease (initial inoculum)
r = apparent infection rate (rate of disease increase)
t = time (duration of epidemic)
e = base of natural logarithm
Implications:
If r is very high, reducing X₀ only delays the epidemic
If r is very high, X₀ must be reduced to very low levels to have significant effect
Reducing r has relatively greater effect on epidemic than reducing X₀
Reducing X₀ makes strategic sense only if r is also low or being reduced
Three ways to reduce disease: (1) Reduce X₀, (2) Reduce r, (3) Reduce duration t

ID: 125 | Question: Why moisture is an important factor for development of plant disease in epidemic form?
=====================================
## Answer

Moisture is the predominant factor in the development of most epidemics caused by fungi (blights, downy mildews, leaf spots, rusts, anthracnoses), bacteria (leaf spots, blights, soft rots), and nematodes.

### Major Effects of Moisture:

* **Effects on Host:** Moisture promotes succulent, lush growth of the host, making it physically more susceptible to pathogen penetration.
* **Effects on Pathogen:**
  * Increases sporulation of fungi and rapid multiplication of bacteria.
  * Favors spore release by many fungi and oozing of bacterial cells.
  * Enables fungal spores to germinate and allows motile zoospores, bacteria, and nematodes to move through water films to reach hosts.
  * High soil moisture directly increases the activity and survival of soil-borne pathogens like *Pythium*, *Phytophthora*, *Rhizoctonia*, and *Plasmodiophora brassicae*.
* **Effects on Disease Cycle:** Different moisture levels influence different events — e.g., *Pyricularia oryzae* sporulates at moderate moisture (85–90%) but release of spores, dissemination, and infections require high moisture (92–100%).
* **Effects on Vector:** High humidity reduces the activity of insect vectors but enhances the activity of fungal and nematode vectors.

ID: 128 | Question: How does the fluctuation of day and night temperature affect epidemic development?
=====================================
## Answer

Temperature directly dictates the geographic distribution and seasonal occurrence of plant disease epidemics.

### Key Temperature Fluctuation Effects:

* **Effects of Temperature on Hosts:**
  * Temperatures higher or lower than the optimum range for the host plant reduce the plant's metabolic activity, making it stressed and predisposed to disease infection.
* **Effects on Pathogen:**
  * Favorable temperature helps polycyclic pathogens complete their lifecycle within a very short time, exponentially increasing inoculum production.

  * Dissemination of spores begins at night and ceases at sunrise.
  * This day/night temperature and light cycle creates periodic peaks of inoculum that drive epidemic cycles.

ID: 129 | Question: [✦ Repeated ×2] Describe briefly the principles of plant disease control
=====================================
## Answer

The 6 principles (APTREE):
1. Avoidance: Avoiding disease by altering planting time or planting in areas where inoculum is ineffective due to environmental conditions. Done through choice of geographical area, time of sowing, field selection, disease-escaping varieties.
2. Protection: Preventing infection by creating a chemical toxic barrier between plants and pathogens. Done through chemical treatment, control of insect vectors, modification of environment.
3. Therapy: Reducing the severity of disease in an already infected individual by chemicals (chemotherapy), tree surgery, heat therapy. E.g., Bordeaux Mixture.
4. Resistance (Resistant Varieties): Preventing infection or reducing effects by managing the host through genetic improvement. Includes selection, hybridization, and mutation for disease resistance.
5. Eradication: Eliminating a pathogen after it is introduced into an area but before it has widely spread. Done through crop rotation, soil treatment, burning infected residues, biological control.
6. Exclusion: Preventing inoculum from entering or establishing in the field. Done through seed treatment, inspection, certification, quarantine, cleaned farm equipment.

ID: 134 | Question: [✦ Repeated ×3] "Biological control is prioritized in disease management program" — justify / What does biological control mean? Discuss the mechanism of antagonism
=====================================
## Answer

### 1. Definition of Biological Control
**Biological control** is the suppression of a plant pathogen population by another organism (referred to as the antagonist/biocontrol agent). It is a population-leveling process in which the population of the antagonist lowers the survival or density of the pathogen. First coined by **Harry Smith**.

### 2. Why Biological Control is Prioritized:
* **Eco-friendly:** Chemical pesticides are highly toxic to non-target beneficial organisms, leave residues, and cause massive soil/water pollution. Biocontrol agents are non-toxic and biodegradable.
* **Pathogen Resistance:** Pathogens frequently develop rapid resistance to single-site chemical fungicides. Development of resistance to multi-site biocontrol mechanisms is highly unlikely.
* **Sustainability:** Chemical pesticides require repeated treatments, while established biocontrol populations can self-replicate and persist in the rhizosphere for years.

### 3. Mechanisms of Antagonism (How BCA Suppresses Pathogen):
* **A. Antibiosis:** Inhibition of one organism by another through the secretion of antibiotics or toxic metabolites.
  * *Example:* *Bacillus cereus* produces *Zwittermicin A* against *Phytophthora* root rot in alfalfa; *Bacillus subtilis* produces *Iturin A* against *Botrytis cinerea*.
* **B. Nutrient Competition:** Direct competition between BCA and pathogen for vital resources like Carbon, Nitrogen, Oxygen, or Iron.
  * *Example:* *Pseudomonas fluorescens* prevents bacterial blotch by competing with *Pseudomonas tolaasii*.
* **C. Mycoparasitism / Hyperparasitism:** The antagonist directly invades and feeds on the pathogen hyphae.
  * *Example:* *Trichoderma harzianum* coils around host hyphae, secretes cell-wall-degrading enzymes (chitinases, glucanases), and penetrates *Fusarium culmorum*.
* **D. Lytic Enzymes:** Release of enzymes (chitinase, β-1,3-glucanase, protease) that directly hydrolyze and rupture pathogen cell walls.
* **E. Siderophore Secretion:** BCA releases high-affinity iron chelators (**siderophores**) that bind free Fe³⁺, making it unavailable to rhizosphere pathogens.
* **F. Induced Systemic Resistance (ISR):** Colonization of roots by PGPRs (*Pseudomonas*, *Trichoderma*) induces plant systemic resistance depending on ethylene and jasmonic acid pathways.
* **G. Cross Protection:** Inoculation with a mild virus strain protects the host against severe virulent strains of the same virus (e.g., Citrus Tristeza Virus).

Mechanism:
P. infestans survives in infected tubers (vegetative mycelium in tubers = dormant inoculum)
Growing potato every year in the same land allows the pathogen to persist and build up inoculum (X₀)
Rotating with a non-host crop (e.g., wheat, rice) for 1–2 seasons starves the pathogen of its host
This reduces initial inoculum (X₀) and delays epidemic onset
Supporting measures:
Sanitation: Remove and destroy infected crop residues and cull piles after harvest (eliminates overwintering inoculum)
Selection: Use certified, disease-free seed tubers
Chemical protection if needed: Mancozeb + Metalaxyl (Ridomil Gold) or Copper Oxychloride (Blitox)
Why this is best: Since late blight is a polycyclic disease, reducing initial inoculum (X₀) combined with removing the substrate from the same field directly breaks the disease cycle and prevents the sigmoid epidemic from initiating.

ID: 137 | Question: [✦ Repeated ×3] Differentiate vertical resistance from horizontal resistance. Which one do you prefer and why?
=====================================
## Answer

### Comparison: Vertical vs. Horizontal Resistance

| Feature | Vertical Resistance (VR) | Horizontal Resistance (HR) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | Complete resistance to some races of a pathogen but not to others. | Partial resistance equally effective against all races of a pathogen. |
| **Gene Basis** | Monogenic or oligogenic (major genes). | Polygenic (minor genes). |
| **Host Specificity** | Race-specific / Host specific. | Race non-specific / Host non-specific. |
| **Effect on Pathogen** | VR does not allow the pathogen to establish in the host — no increase of disease. | HR allows the pathogen to establish but decreases the rate of disease increase. |
| **Epidemiological Implication** | Epidemic cannot develop until breakdown by a new pathogen race. | Epidemic development depends on the level of HR and environmental conditions. |
| **Durability** | Less durable — can be rapidly overcome by new virulent races. | More durable. |

### Preference & Justification:
We strongly prefer **Horizontal Resistance (HR)** in broad-scale agricultural systems for the following reasons:
1. **Durability:** Since it is governed by multiple minor genes (polygenic), the pathogen cannot overcome it with a single nucleotide mutation. Thus, it lasts much longer in the field.
2. **Stability:** It provides stable, race-non-specific protection. Unlike the "boom and bust" cycle of Vertical Resistance, there is no threat of sudden complete crop failure when new pathogen races evolve.
3. **Epidemic Suppression:** Although it allows the pathogen to establish at minor, non-damaging levels, it directly slows down the rate of epidemic progress ($r$), keeping disease severity below economic injury levels.

ID: 138 | Question: "Mixed cropping reduces disease incidence" — briefly discuss the reasons
=====================================
## Answer

Reduces the density of any single host species in a field, lowering the chance of rapid pathogen spread from plant to plant
Acts similarly to crop rotation in disrupting the disease cycle
Genetically diverse host population reduces the effectiveness of host-specific pathogens
Physical barriers between susceptible plants reduce wind and rain-splash dissemination of inoculum
Reduces genetic uniformity — genetically uniform (monoculture) host plants are most susceptible to epidemics; mixed cropping increases genetic diversity, helping to develop epidemic resistance

ID: 140 | Question: Why do resistant varieties sometimes breakdown in the field?
=====================================
## Answer

From the content on Vertical Resistance (VR) and epidemiological implications:
Vertical resistance provides complete resistance to some races of a pathogen but not to others
When a new race of the pathogen evolves (through mutation or genetic recombination), this new race can overcome the VR of the previously resistant variety
This is called breakdown of resistance
Epidemics cannot develop in VR varieties until breakdown by a new pathogen race
VR does not allow the pathogen to establish, but once a virulent race appears, the host becomes completely susceptible and epidemic can develop even under favorable environment

ID: 141 | Question: Discuss the importance of host resistance within Integrated Disease Management (IDM)
=====================================
## Answer

Host resistance is one of the 6 basic types of control measures in plant disease management
Prevents infection or reduces the effect of infection by managing the host through genetic improvement
Vertical resistance reduces the effective amount of incoming inoculum (avirulent races cannot infect)
Horizontal resistance decreases the rate of disease development (r) for all races
Resistant varieties are the most economical, environmentally safe control measure
They reduce reliance on chemical fungicides, reduce cost of production, and provide season-long protection
Within IDM, resistant varieties form the backbone while cultural, biological and chemical methods supplement

ID: 142 | Question: Analyze how cultural measures contribute to climate-smart disease management
=====================================
## Answer

Crop rotation: reduces inoculum levels without any chemical input — sustainable
Selection of planting date: avoids pathogen peak activity periods — adapts to changing climate patterns
Adjusted seeding rate and canopy density: reduces humid microclimate within canopy — reduces disease favorable conditions
Balanced fertilizer application: maintains plant health without promoting excessive susceptible growth
Sanitation: (burning/plowing residues) reduces overwintering inoculum
Irrigation management: avoiding overhead irrigation to reduce leaf wetness, which is critical since moisture is the predominant epidemic factor
Trap crops and cover crops: reduce pathogen movement and provide physical barriers
These methods collectively reduce initial inoculum (X₀) and rate of epidemic (r) without environmental contamination — making them climate-smart.
